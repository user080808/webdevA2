World War 2

The history, the key players, and their strengths and weaknesses

The participants

The Axis powers

Formed by the Tripartite Pact in 1940, the Axis powers consisted of three nations, those being Nazi Germany, Fascist Italy, and the Japanese Empire. They were laregly driven by expansionist and militaristic ideologies, and sought to change the geopolitical landscape to shape their interests. They opposed the Allies in World War 2.

The key leaders of the nations were:

Adolf Hitler, of Nazi Germany

Benito Mussolini, of Fascist Italy

Emperor Hirohito, of the Japanese Empire

The Allied powers

Formed in opposition to the aggresive expansion of the Axis, the Allied powers had significantly more nations than the Axis, but the three most powerful were Great Britain, the United States, and the Soviet Union (Often called the "Big Three"). Some other significant nations included Australia, India and China.

The key leaders of the "Big Three" were:

Winston Churchill, of Great Britain

Franklin Delano Roosevelt, of the United States

Joseph Stalin, of the Soviet Union

The timeline

Invasion of Poland

On September 1 1939, Germany invades Poland, joined by the Soviet Union (Which was neutral to Germany at the time due to the Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact, or German-Soviet Non-Aggression Pact).
Britain and France soon declared war on Germany, marking the start of World War 2 in Europe.
The Phoney war would soon follow, characterised by a build up and preparation of military forces.

The Battle and Fall of France

Germany utilises blitzkrieg tactics to conquer Belgium, Luxembourg and the Netherlands, before invading France on 10 May 1940.
Although France had a larger army and air force as well as the support of the British Expeditionary Force and Royal Air Force (RAF), they were unable to resist the attack and eventually retreated to Dunkirk where evacuation took place.
The Dunkirk evacuation was successful in rescuing several British and French soldiers, while France surrendered on June 22. France became occupied by Germany and Japan occupies French Indochina in September.

The Blitz and Battle of Britain

With France defeated, Germany now turned its attention to Britain, with the German Air Force (Luftwaffe) carrying out large scale attacks against Britain's RAF in a bid to achieve air superiority.
Later, the Germans turned to bombing British cities in a campaign known as the Blitz. It lasted from September 1940 to May 1941, although bombing continued throughout the war.
Though the Blitz causes huge losses of life and great damage to houses, docks, warehouses and factories, the British did not surrender and carried on the war effort.

Operation Barbarossa

Violating the Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact, Germany launches a massive invasion into the Soviet Union on June 22 1940, drawing away air forces from the Battle of the Atlantic and land forces from other theatres of war.
Although the Germans enjoyed considerable success in the early stages, they were unable to overwhelm the USSR fast enough and were halted by the harsh winter.
The Soviets exploited this advantage to their benefit by reorganising the Red Army. This allowed them to better resist the German forces.

Attack on Pearl Harbor

On December 7 1941, Japan launches a surprise attack on Pearl Harbor, disabling much of the US naval fleet stationed there.
Over 2,000 American personnel were killed and 18 ships were destroyed, along with five battleships. However, Japan did not destroy any aircraft carriers.
Roosevelt would deliver his Day of Infamy speech the next day, rallying the country and declaring war on Japan with the United Kingdom. Later on December 11, Germany and Italy declared war on the United States, even though they had no formal obligation to do so under the Tripartite Pact.

The Battle of Midway

In June 1942, the United States and Japan clashed at the Midway Atoll.
Japan suffered a decisive defeat. They lost four of their aircraft carriers and nearly three-quarters of their experienced pilots.
With their naval and offensive capabilities now severely limited, the United States goes on the offensive.

The Battle of Stalingrad

On July 17 1942, the Germans attacked Stalingrad, with the Soviets launching a counterattack on August 1942.
The Red Army launches Operation Uranus on November 19 1942, a counteroffensive to encircle the German forces. It is successful, and approximately 290,000 troops were trapped on 23 November 1942.
Having exhausted their food and ammunition, the Germans surrendered to the Soviets on 2 February 1943, marking the end of the battle. It has been regarded by many to be the turning point in World War 2.

The Battle of Kursk

On July 5 1943, Germany attacks the Soviet city of Kursk, resulting in one of the largest tank battles in history.
By 23 August 1943 however, the Soviets were able to halt and counter the attacks. Hitler cancels the offensive later, marking a Soviet victory.
This was the last major German offensive on the Eastern Front, with Germany now on the defensive and the Soviet Union on the offensive.

Operation Overlord: D-day

As the Germans and Soviets fought, the rest of the Allies sought to liberate France and open a second front in Europe, codenamed Operation Overlord, also known as D-day.
D-day was chosen to take place on 6 June 1944, at the beaches of Normandy. It was the largest amphibious assault and a massive success, owing to overwhelming resources and effective planning and leadership.
From there, the Allies established a foothold in France and began a slow crawl to liberate it from German control.

Allied victory in Europe

By January 1945, the Soviets had encircled Berlin, and Germany was on the verge of total military collapse.
In April 1945, the Soviets launched an offensive into Berlin. Facing imminent defeat, Hitler commits suicide on 30 April 1945.
Germany surrenders to the Allies on 8 May 1945, declaring an Allied victory in Europe.

Atomic bombing and defeat of Japan

By May 1945, the Allies had successfully liberated most of Japan's territory from their control.
The United States then drops an atomic bomb, "Little Boy", on 6 August 1945, over Hiroshima. A second atomic bomb, "Fat Man", was dropped over Nagasaki on 9 August 1945.
Japan surrenders to the Allies on 14 August 1945, declaring an Allied victory in the Asia-Pacific and marking the end of World War 2.

The strengths of the Allies

The United States

Economic and industrial power

Although the United States had vast amounts of resources, it was not ready for war and its people strongly opposed joining the war. However, the Pearl Harbor attack changed public opinion and soon the United States quickly mobilised for war.

From May 1942 onwards, the United States supplied the Allies with huge amounts of food, raw materials, weapons, troops, and other essentials for war. In a summit meeting in 1943, Stalin proposed a toast: "To American production, without which this war would have been lost."

For the duration of the war, US factories produced 250,000 aircraft, 90,000 tanks, 350 naval destroyers, 200 submarines and 5,600 merchant ships. By 1944, the United States was producing almost half of the weapons being made globally - more than twice the production of Germany and Japan combined.

During the Battle of the Atlantic, US shipyards constructed cargo ships, called Liberty ships, to help transport supplies to the Aliies. In 1943, three ships were completed daily. Soon, the United States was building more ships than the German U-boats could sink.

In the Asia-Pacific, the United States supplied the Guomindang (GMD) in China with money, weapons and other aid to fight the Japanese. As the war progressed, resistance movements across Asia such as the Malayan People's Anti-Japanese Army and Viet Minh would receive backing from the United States.

Island-hopping in the Asia-Pacific

By the time the Japanese attacked Pearl Harbor, they had already built up a large empire in Asia and the Pacific. Even after the United States victory at Midway, the Allies faced a long and costly campaign against the Japanese and their island strongholds.

Thus, they came up with the idea of Island-hopping. Instead of trying to capture every single island, the Allies would target weaker, less-defended islands instead which supplied the stronger islands.

This approach was successful, as the well-defended islands gradually weakened over time. It allowed the Allies to conserve their resources and manpowers and avoid costly battles.

The Atomic Bomb

The United States' usage of the atomic bomb was another crucial factor in the Allied victory. Even though they were winning, Japanese resistance would cost them countless casualties and recources.

The usage of the atomic bombs without warning swiftly crushed Japanese morale and made them surrender.

Britain

Strategic position and perseverance

When Britain became the last country standing in Europe of 1940, many politicians believed they should make a deal or an agreement wiht Germany, which could have costed Britain its military, particularly the Royal Navy.

However, Churchill refused to do so, preferring that Britain conntinues to fight and resist Germany. This would prove fundamental to Germany's defeat, as Britain's location was a valuable asset.

Fighting over home ground gave the British the advantage of short flight times and quicker refuelling and rearming. Britain was also able to tap into their large reserves of spare parts for repair work, allowing them to replace their losses more quickly than Germany, which had stretched it supply lines.

The country became a substantial military base. Allied troops from all over the world such as from Canada, the Carribean and the United States were stationed at Britain, which made the D-day landings of 1944 and the Allied bombing campaigns against Germany possible.

Advanced technology

Britain's Royal Air Force (RAF) had excellent fighter aircraft such as the Supermarine Spitfire and the Hawker Hurricane, which were able to put up a strong fight against Germany's newly developed aircraft.

In addition, the British also developed the new technology of radar, which was used to detect and locate incoming enemy aircraft. This allowed them to detect German air raids ahead of time, direct aircraft to intercept them, and manage battles effectively.

Colonial manpower and supplies

Britain's survival was instrumental in the war against Japan, as surrendering also meant giving up its many colonies in Asia and giving Japan much less resistance. As it was, the Japanese fought the armies and navies of Britain, Australia, New Zealand and around 120,000 African troops from British colonies alongside US troops.

Moreover, the British colonies included India, and its immense manpower and resources. India contributed over 2.5 million men and a staggering 80% of its wealth to the Allied war effort.

The Soviet Union

Reorganisation and reforms

When the German invasion was halted by the Russian winter in 1941, Stalin and his military leaders used the time to reform the Red Army. German tactics were copied over and new strategies and ideas were implemented.

Officers in the Red Army were given greater freedom to act. Stalin banned Communist Party officials from interfering with military decisions.

Specialist tank armies and air force were created, along with the invention and development of new weapons such as the T-34 tank, which could be mass produced.

Harsh discipline was used. For example, Stalin issued Order 227 in July 1942, known as "Not a step back". The army was ordered to fight and die for every bit of Russian soil.

Radios were put into tanks and aircraft to enable better communication. Specialist units were also created to listen in and disrupt German radio signals.

The Soviet War Economy

To ensure production kept up with the high demand, harsh measure were enacted. Adults received no food unless they contributed to the war effort in any way. Forced labour was used and the secret police kept close control of the population.

This enabled the Soviet Union to produce vast amounts of weapons, with production surpassing Germany in 1942. By 1943, the Soviets were producing one and a half times as many aircraft and twice as many tanks as Germany.

The Soviets also shifted its major industrial complexes away to the Urals, Siberia and Kazakhstan. Twenty-five million workers were forced to migrate east which would later contribute to the increased war production.

Contributing to the Asia-Pacific Theatre of War

After the defeat of Germany, the Soviet Union declared war on Japan and invaded Manchuria, as agreed at the Tehran Conference and Yalta Conference.

The Soviets were able to defeat the Kwantung army at Manchuria and occupy northern Korea, which contributed to Japan's decision to surrender to the Allies.

The weaknesses of the Axis

Germany

Hitler's mistakes

Over the course of the war, the Germans made several critical mistakes, largely due to an inefficient command structure. German commanders had to constantly report back to Hitler who regularly intervened in military decisions.

While hitler may be a shrewd politician, he was never a senior military commander and had very little command experience, let alone any at the highest level.

One key mistake was during the Dunkirk evacuation. Hitler ordered his panzer divisions to halt their advance to the evacuating British Expeditionary Force and French army. This gave the British and French troops ample time to evacuate to Britain, which was crucial for British morale.

Hitler would blunder again when he placed Germany in a two-front war, by failing to defeat Britain yet proceeding to invade the Soviet Union.

This prevented him from concentrating his efforts and resources on a single front, causing the military to be stretched thin and gradually worn down by the Allies.

Another key mistake was the battle of Stalingrad. As the Soviets stubbornly resisted the Germans, many commanders wanted to withdraw, as the city was ot particularly valuable from a strategic point of view.

However, Hitler overruled them. To Hitler, taking Stalingrad was a matter of pride, as the city was named after Stalin. As a result, he committed countless troops to a pointless battle that was eventually lost.

Furthermore, Hitler erroneously decided to declare war on the United States, even though he had no obligation to do so under the terms of the Tripartite Pact.

This would bring the United States, which was previously neutral, into the European theatre of war. Germany would now have to contend with the immsense resources and military might brought about by one of the most industiralised countries in the world.

Poor planning

In addition to Hitler's mistakes, the Germans also failed to plan well in their land, air, and naval powers.

In terms of land power, the Germans overengineered their tanks. While they were more advanced and powerful than Allied tanks, they were also overcomplicated, unreliable, and were difficult to mass produce and maintain. Allied tanks on the other hand, such as the T-34 and M4 Sherman, were made for mass production, reliability and ease of use. This meant the Allies could easily replace their losses, while the Germans could not.

In terms of naval power, the Germans were highly effective in their usage of U-boats. However, their usage of surface ships was much less so. They entered the war with a largely weak and incomplete naval fleet compared to Britain's Royal Navy, and put too many resources into building huge battleships such as the Bismarck which were quickly disabled early into the war. They also failed to build aircraft carriers which the US and Japan used to dominate the seas.

In terms of air power, the Germans never made heavy bombers such as the RAF's Lancaster or the US' B-17. They stuck to medium bombers such as the Heinkel He 111 which were largely inferior in terms of the damage range they had. While they also had a wide variety of aircraft produced, this caused inefficient supply lines and a shortage of spare parts, in addition to their inefficient supply of oil and failure to invest in radar technology. These problems would plague the Luftwaffe later in the war.

Japan

Interservice rivalry vetween the Army and Navy

Even before the war, the Imperial Japanese Army (IJA) and Imperial Japanese Navy (IJN) had a long-standing conflict and rivalry with each other. Both had different strategies and ideas on how to seize territory and resources for the Empire, with the IJA prioritising northern expansion into China and the USSR, and the IJN prioritising southern expansion into the Dutch East Indies and the Pacific.

When war broke out, the rivalry prevented the IJN and IJA from sharing resources and exchanging military intelligence, much less coordinate attacks and defences against the Allies. In addition, their refusal to cooperate caused them to manufacture their own air force. This meant the already weak Japanese economy was wasting even more resources building two different sets of equipment for the Army's air force and the Navy's air force.

Instances of their rivalry included the IJA's refusal to provide troop support to the IJN for an invasion of Australia, and the IJA's refusal to commit major reinforcements to the Pacific Islands. Both sides also disagreed on where to set Japan's defensive perimeter against the Allied forces.

Overstretched empire

Although Japan managed to acquire vast amounts of territory and resources, they were unable to effectively utilise them due to several reasons.

One of those reasons was how spread out the Empire was. In many areas, rail and road communications were poor, resulting in difficulty delivering raw materials and workers efficiently to the required places.

It also meant that resources had to be transported by sea, which was much more challenging and risky. This made the Japanese highly-suspectible to the island-hopping strategy employed by the Allies, which allowed them to establish bases on key islands while draining the resources of other ones.

Poor planning

Much like Germany, Japan failed to plan properly for their military. In particular, they failed to understand that war at sea had changed.

While Japan managed to damage US battleships at Pearl Harbour, they did not destroy any aircraft carriers which contributed to the US' quick recovery from the attack.

Although Japan built aircraft carriers and trained naval pilots, they were extremely slow in doing so, resulting in an irreplaceble carrier fleet that was lost at Midway. Similar to Germany as well, they overinvested in battleships such as the Yamato which ate up huge amounts of resources yet achieved little and were quickly sunk.

They were also unable to utilise their submarines effectively. They mostly used them to attack battleships instead of merchant ships, which were much more easier targets and a major opportunity to target the Allied supply lines. The Allies on the other hand, targeted Japanese merchant ships as well along with their navy.

This resulted in a huge reduction of Japan's merchant fleet, reduced to one-ninth of its pre-Pearl Harbor capacity by the time they surrendered, and only half of the supplies shipped out was received by their defences, starving and weakening them until they were easy pickings.

Lack of local support

The Japanese also failed to properly use their empire to the fullest, due to their brutal treatment of the peoples they conquered. Locals were abused and thousands were forced into slave labour. Millions of civilians died under Japanese rule.

As a result, the locals rebelled against their rule in any way they could, with many joining large-scale resistance movements across their home countries.

Examples include Mao Zedong's Chinese Communist Party in China, Ho Chi Minh's Viet Minh in Vietnam, and the Malayan People's Anti-Japanese Army in Malaya. These groups mostly fought using guerrilla warfare and were effective in harassing Japanese forces, disrupting supply lines and engaging in sabotage.
